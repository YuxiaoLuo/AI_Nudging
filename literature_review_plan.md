# Literature Review Plan for "Talking Shoppers Into Buying? Experimental Evidence on LLM-Driven AI Nudges"

## Review goal
Build a literature base that clarifies how **LLM-driven conversational nudges** differ from **algorithm-generated recommendation badges** and related forms of digital choice architecture on e-commerce platforms.

## Core distinction to investigate
### Algorithm-generated recommendation badges
Examples:
- Best Seller
- Recommended for You
- Sponsored
- Popular Choice
- Top Pick

Typical properties:
- interface-level and visually embedded
- static or semi-static at a given moment
- usually low-interaction
- often one-shot exposure
- nudge works through salience, ranking, highlighting, or heuristic cues
- influence is usually indirect and lightweight

### LLM-driven conversational nudges
Examples:
- chatbot says which product to buy and why
- chatbot compares alternatives and steers attention
- assistant adapts to stated preferences in real time
- assistant sequences information through dialogue

Typical properties:
- conversation-level rather than badge-level
- interactive and iterative
- adaptive across turns
- can infer and reshape preferences during dialogue
- higher perceived social presence / agency
- more opaque in recommendation logic
- potentially stronger persuasive and confidence-shaping effects
- nudge is embedded in advice, explanation, framing, and turn-by-turn guidance

## Working conceptual claim
LLM conversational nudges are not just stronger recommendation badges. They represent a **qualitatively different form of choice architecture** because they combine personalization, dialogue, adaptive framing, and algorithmic opacity in ways that can change both **what consumers choose** and **how they arrive at the choice**.

## Priority literature buckets
1. Digital nudging and online choice architecture
2. Recommender systems and recommendation cues in e-commerce
3. Conversational agents / conversational commerce
4. AI transparency, explainability, and algorithmic opacity
5. Consumer decision quality, confidence, and cognitive load
6. Trust in algorithmic or AI advice

## Screening criteria
Prioritize papers that are about:
- e-commerce or online shopping contexts
- recommendation systems or product guidance
- AI, algorithms, transparency, or explainability
- consumer choice, purchase, decision quality, trust, confidence, or cognitive effort
- experiments, field experiments, or strong conceptual framing
