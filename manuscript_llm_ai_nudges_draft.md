# Talking Shoppers Into Buying? Experimental Evidence on LLM-Driven AI Nudges

## Abstract
Large language models are increasingly embedded into online shopping interfaces, but their behavioral role is not yet well understood within digital-nudging research. We theorize LLM-based conversational shopping assistants as a distinct form of AI nudge that operates through adaptive dialogue rather than static interface placement alone. Relative to traditional UI-based nudges, conversational AI should improve decision support by increasing perceived personalization, reducing cognitive load, and strengthening choice confidence, while also reducing perceived transparency because the recommendation process becomes less visible and less easily reconstructed. We propose a three-condition online experiment that compares an LLM conversational nudge, a traditional UI-based nudge, and a no-nudge control in a headphone-shopping task. The model predicts that conversational AI will increase purchase likelihood through a support route while producing a tradeoff in perceived decision quality through competing confidence and transparency pathways. By positioning conversational AI as dialogic choice architecture rather than merely a stronger recommendation cue, the paper contributes to digital-nudging, recommendation-agent, and conversational-commerce research and clarifies why AI shopping assistance may simultaneously feel more helpful and less inspectable.

## Introduction
Online shopping platforms increasingly embed large language models into recommendation and assistance interfaces, creating a new form of digital choice architecture in which consumers are guided through interactive conversation rather than static interface cues alone. Prior research shows that digital nudges such as rankings, labels, and recommendation cues can meaningfully alter search, comparison, and purchase behavior, while recommendation-agent research shows that explanations, neutrality, and disclosure shape how advisory technologies are evaluated (Häubl & Trifts 2000; Senecal & Nantel 2004; Ursu 2018; Wang & Benbasat 2007; Wang et al. 2018; Wang & Wang 2019). Yet LLM-based shopping assistants do more than highlight products or present a recommendation. They can solicit preferences, summarize tradeoffs, personalize guidance in real time, and steer users through a dynamic exchange that may feel both more helpful and less inspectable than traditional interface nudges.

This shift creates an important theoretical gap. Existing digital-nudging and recommendation literatures provide a strong baseline for understanding interface-level influence and advisory-system design, but they do not fully explain whether conversational AI operates as merely a stronger recommendation cue or as a qualitatively different architecture of influence. Compared with static UI nudges, conversational AI may improve decision support by making guidance feel more personalized, reducing cognitive burden, and increasing confidence in the final choice. At the same time, conversational guidance may reduce transparency because consumers cannot easily determine why a recommendation was produced, how tradeoffs were weighted, or whether commercial priorities shaped the advice. The relevant question is therefore not only whether conversational AI increases purchase likelihood, but whether it changes the consumer decision process itself in ways that create a tradeoff between decision support and inspectability.

To address that question, this paper theorizes LLM conversational assistants as a distinct form of AI nudge in online shopping. The central argument is that conversational AI changes choice architecture through adaptive dialogue rather than interface placement alone and that this shift generates two competing process routes. On the benefit side, conversational guidance should increase perceived personalization, reduce cognitive load, and strengthen choice confidence. On the risk side, it should reduce perceived transparency in the recommendation process. We test this argument in a three-condition experiment comparing an LLM conversational nudge, a traditional UI-based nudge, and a no-nudge control condition. In doing so, the paper contributes to digital-nudging and conversational-commerce research by showing that the central issue raised by conversational AI is not simply stronger persuasive effectiveness, but a different balance between decision support and opacity in digital choice architecture.

## Conceptualization and Focal Comparison
In this paper, LLM-driven conversational AI nudges are defined as platform-mediated choice interventions delivered through interactive natural-language dialogue, in which recommendations, framing, sequencing, and emphasis are generated or adapted by an LLM in ways that steer user decision processes without restricting the available options. This form of nudge is conversational rather than purely visual, interactive rather than one-shot, adaptive rather than fixed, algorithmically generated rather than fully manually scripted, and comparatively opaque in its underlying recommendation logic.

The focal comparison in the study is therefore not between AI and non-AI in the abstract, but among three distinct forms of shopping guidance. In the conversational-nudge condition, participants receive shopping guidance through a chatbot-style assistant that recommends, compares, frames, and steers options in natural language. In the UI-based nudge condition, participants encounter static or semi-static nudges such as recommendation badges, highlighted placement, or similar interface cues. In the control condition, participants browse the same product environment without explicit nudging cues. This comparison structure matters because it allows the study to isolate whether conversational AI changes consumer decision processes differently from a more traditional interface-level nudge rather than merely asking whether any recommendation aid changes behavior relative to no aid at all.

## Theoretical Background
Research on digital choice architecture has long shown that relatively simple interface structures can shape consumer behavior by altering attention allocation, search order, and comparison efficiency. Rankings, labels, recommendation cues, and interactive decision aids can reduce search costs and guide consumers toward focal options, even when the underlying alternatives remain unchanged (Häubl & Trifts 2000; Senecal & Nantel 2004; Ursu 2018). A related stream on recommendation agents extends this logic by showing that advisory technologies are evaluated not only on usefulness, but also on the explanations they provide, the neutrality they signal, and the integrity they appear to embody (Xiao & Benbasat 2007; Wang & Benbasat 2007; Wang et al. 2018; Wang & Wang 2019). These literatures establish that digital guidance can influence both what consumers choose and how they interpret the technologies assisting them.

LLM-based conversational assistants extend this established logic in an important way. Rather than merely signaling a recommended option or displaying a preconfigured explanation, they move recommendation support into adaptive, sequential dialogue. Conversational assistants can ask follow-up questions, incorporate user-stated priorities, reformulate tradeoffs in natural language, and present a recommendation as if it were the result of an ongoing advisory exchange. This makes the shopping aid more responsive to the user, but it may also make the basis of the recommendation harder to reconstruct after the fact. Conversational AI is therefore better understood not simply as a stronger badge or smarter recommender, but as a more dialogic form of choice architecture whose benefits and risks must be theorized jointly.

The central benefit route begins with perceived personalization. Because a conversational assistant can gather richer preference input and present advice in language that appears tailored to the shopper's goals, consumers should experience the guidance as more individualized than static UI nudges. Perceived personalization in this context refers to the consumer's perception that the recommendation guidance is specifically adapted to their own needs, goals, and preference structure rather than offered as a generic platform suggestion. Existing conversational-shopping work is consistent with this expectation. Chung et al. (2020) show that personalization meaningfully shapes consumer responses to voice-shopping recommendations, while Chen et al. (2021) frame the conversational agent as a decision aid whose effectiveness depends on how well its interaction fits the shopper's task. Greater perceived personalization should, in turn, reduce cognitive load by narrowing the set of plausible options and making attribute tradeoffs easier to process. Cognitive load here refers to the mental effort required to process alternatives, compare attributes, and arrive at a choice in the shopping task. Prior work on interactive decision aids supports this logic: Häubl and Trifts (2000) show that decision-aid tools can improve the efficiency of product search and comparison, and Chen et al. (2021) suggests that conversational assistance can play a similar task-support role in shopping settings. As the task feels more manageable, consumers should become more confident in the choice they make. Conversational AI can thus improve the felt quality of shopping support not merely by increasing salience, but by changing how consumers experience the task of evaluating alternatives.

The central risk route concerns transparency. The same adaptive and fluent qualities that make conversational guidance feel useful may also reduce the visibility of how recommendations are produced. Unlike a static badge or ranking, conversational advice does not necessarily reveal which attributes were prioritized, how the recommendation was derived, or whether commercial motives shaped the guidance. Prior recommendation-agent and disclosure research suggests that such opacity matters because explanation, neutrality, and perceived integrity shape how consumers evaluate advisory technologies (Wang & Benbasat 2007; Wang et al. 2018; Wang & Wang 2019; Luo et al. 2019). In the present paper, perceived transparency refers to the extent to which the basis of the recommendation feels understandable and reconstructable after the fact. The resulting theoretical expectation is therefore a tradeoff: conversational AI may improve purchase support and subjective confidence while also weakening consumers' ability to inspect and evaluate the basis of the recommendation.

## Hypotheses and Model
The research model follows directly from this tradeoff logic. Relative to UI-based nudges and control, LLM conversational nudges should increase perceived personalization because the guidance is experienced as more responsive to the shopper's stated goals and constraints. Greater perceived personalization should lower cognitive load, and lower cognitive load should increase choice confidence. Confidence is especially relevant in shopping environments characterized by attribute complexity and incomplete consumer knowledge, because consumers who experience the decision process as manageable should feel more certain that they are arriving at an acceptable choice. Choice confidence therefore refers to the consumer's subjective certainty that the selected option is the right or appropriate choice among the considered alternatives. Confidence, in turn, should support higher perceived decision quality because consumers often evaluate their decisions partly through whether the final choice feels justified and well grounded. At the same time, confidence is not conceptually identical to decision quality. Perceived decision quality captures the broader evaluative judgment that the final decision is sound, appropriate, and well justified given the available information and decision context. In the present model, confidence is therefore treated as a downstream consequence of the benefit route rather than as a substitute for evaluative quality. At the same time, conversational nudges should reduce perceived transparency because the recommendation process is less visible and less easily reconstructed than in a static interface condition. Lower transparency should weaken perceived decision quality by making it harder for consumers to judge whether the recommendation was well founded. Conversational AI may nevertheless increase purchase likelihood directly because a more fluent and structured decision process can reduce friction and make purchase action more likely even when transparency is imperfect.

Taken together, these predictions imply that the effect of conversational AI on decision quality is not uniformly positive or negative. Rather, it should be shaped jointly by a positive personalization-to-confidence route and a negative transparency route. The core theoretical claim is therefore that LLM conversational nudges do not merely intensify existing interface effects; they reconfigure digital choice architecture by combining stronger decision support with lower inspectability.

The resulting hypotheses are as follows:

**H1:** Relative to UI-based nudges and control, LLM conversational nudges increase perceived personalization.

**H2:** Greater perceived personalization is associated with lower cognitive load during product choice.

**H3:** Lower cognitive load is associated with higher choice confidence.

**H4:** Higher choice confidence is associated with higher perceived decision quality.

**H5:** Relative to UI-based nudges and control, LLM conversational nudges decrease perceived transparency.

**H6:** Lower perceived transparency is associated with lower perceived decision quality.

**H7:** Relative to UI-based nudges and control, LLM conversational nudges increase purchase likelihood.

**H8:** The effect of LLM conversational nudges on perceived decision quality is jointly shaped by a positive personalization-to-confidence path and a negative transparency path.

## Method
Study 1 uses a three-condition between-subject online experiment comparing an LLM conversational nudge, a traditional UI-based nudge, and a no-nudge control condition.

### Participants and design
Participants are adult consumers recruited from a general online panel such as Prolific or CloudResearch. Because the study does not require product expertise, the key inclusion criterion is simply that participants can complete an ordinary English-language online shopping task. A target sample of roughly 450 to 600 completed responses is appropriate because it would provide approximately 150 to 200 participants per condition, which is a more credible range for estimating the proposed condition differences and process paths than a small pilot-style design. Responses that are incomplete, duplicated, or demonstrably low effort are excluded through prespecified quality checks rather than post hoc trimming.

All participants read the same shopping scenario: they need a pair of wireless over-ear headphones for commuting, work, and casual listening and have a budget cap of $180. They then evaluate the same six fictional headphone options, which vary on price, battery life, weight, active noise cancellation, microphone quality, comfort rating, and user rating.

The conversational-nudge condition presents a semi-structured assistant that asks a short sequence of preference questions and then recommends the balanced focal option, `VeroWave M6`, with brief comparative reasoning. The UI-based nudge condition presents the same focal option with static interface cues such as a `Best Match` badge, first-position placement, and a short recommendation note. The control condition presents the same product set without explicit recommendation cues. Holding the assortment constant while varying only the mode of guidance helps isolate whether conversational assistance changes the consumer decision process differently from a traditional UI nudge.

### Procedure
After reading the shopping scenario, participants are randomly assigned to one of the three shopping environments. They review the product set, interact with the assistant or interface cues as applicable, indicate which option they would choose, and then report purchase likelihood. The post-task instrument then measures perceived personalization, cognitive load, choice confidence, perceived transparency, and perceived decision quality, followed by secondary items on trust and satisfaction, manipulation checks, and a compact set of control variables. This sequence keeps the behavioral and evaluative responses close to the shopping task while leaving the control and verification items until the end of the survey.

### Stimuli
To reduce brand-specific confounds while keeping the shopping task realistic, the study uses fictional but plausible headphone brands. A compact six-option set is sufficient because it creates meaningful comparison without producing an unwieldy choice screen. The product set includes one option emphasizing low price, one emphasizing battery life, one emphasizing active noise cancellation, one emphasizing call quality, one emphasizing comfort, and one balanced midrange option. The balanced midrange option, `VeroWave M6`, remains the focal recommendation in both the conversational and UI-nudge conditions so that the substantive recommendation is held constant across treatments.

A first-pass product set can be structured as follows. `Auralite A1` emphasizes value at approximately $89 but offers weaker noise cancellation and call quality. `Auralite B3` emphasizes battery life at approximately $149 with 50 hours of playback but only moderate comfort. `NordSound C5` emphasizes noise cancellation at approximately $179 with strong commuting performance but higher weight. `NordSound D2` emphasizes call quality at approximately $159 with the strongest microphone score but only average battery life. `VeroWave H4` emphasizes comfort at approximately $169 with the best comfort rating and low weight but only midrange microphone quality. `VeroWave M6` functions as the balanced focal option at approximately $139, offering solid but non-dominant performance across sound quality, comfort, battery life, microphone quality, and noise cancellation.

The same attribute columns are displayed in every condition: price, battery life, weight, active noise cancellation, microphone quality, comfort rating, and user rating. In the conversational condition, the assistant asks which attribute matters most, asks about the participant's primary use context, and asks whether budget strictness is more important than maximizing features. It then recommends `VeroWave M6` and briefly compares it with `NordSound C5` and `Auralite B3`. In the UI-based nudge condition, `VeroWave M6` is positioned first, marked with a `Best Match` badge, and accompanied by a short static note such as `Balanced choice for commuting, work, and everyday listening`. The control condition presents the same six products without recommendation cues.

### Measures
The primary outcomes are purchase likelihood and perceived decision quality. Purchase likelihood is measured with items such as whether the participant would be likely to purchase the selected option, whether they would seriously consider buying it, and whether the probability of purchase is high. Perceived decision quality captures whether the final choice feels sound, appropriate, and well justified, using items about making a high-quality decision, arriving at a sound overall decision, and selecting an option that appropriately fits the participant's needs.

The core process measures capture perceived personalization, cognitive load, choice confidence, and perceived transparency. Perceived personalization items assess whether the guidance felt tailored, whether the shopping aid took the participant's priorities into account, and whether the recommendation felt personalized rather than generic. Cognitive load items capture mental effort, cognitive demand, and whether the task felt mentally taxing. Choice confidence items assess whether participants feel confident, certain, and relatively doubt-free about the option they selected. Perceived transparency items assess whether participants understand why the product was recommended, whether the basis of the recommendation was clear, and whether they could reconstruct the logic behind the guidance they received.

Secondary outcome measures include trust in the shopping aid and satisfaction with the guidance process. Manipulation checks verify whether the shopping aid felt conversational, adaptive, AI-driven, and visibly explanatory, while avoiding direct redundancy with the substantive mediator variables. The compact control set includes product-category familiarity, prior use of AI shopping assistants, general trust in AI, need for cognition through a short validated subset, and shopping involvement. Unless later scale selection indicates otherwise, a seven-point Likert-type format is adequate for the first-pass instrument, with purchase likelihood measured on a seven-point unlikely-to-likely scale.

### Manipulation checks and controls
The manipulation checks verify that the three conditions were experienced as intended without collapsing into the substantive mediators. At a minimum, the instrument assesses whether the shopping aid felt conversational, whether the guidance adapted to the participant's inputs, whether AI played a meaningful role in the recommendation process, and whether the logic behind the recommendation appeared visibly presented. The final check requires careful handling because it sits close to the transparency construct. Its role therefore remains diagnostic rather than substitutive: it is useful for verifying the manipulation, but it is not treated as the same construct as perceived transparency in the main model.

The control set remains deliberately compact. Product-category familiarity is important because more knowledgeable consumers may experience lower cognitive load and rely less on external guidance. Prior use of AI shopping assistants and general trust in AI are relevant because they may shift baseline receptivity to conversational recommendations. Need for cognition is included through a short validated subset because consumers who enjoy effortful thinking may respond differently to simplified guidance. Shopping involvement is also retained because a more involving decision context may intensify attention to both personalization benefits and transparency concerns. Keeping this control set theory-justified and restrained makes the design easier to defend empirically.

## Analysis Plan
The empirical analysis focuses on two headline dependent variables, purchase likelihood and perceived decision quality, while testing whether the conversational condition operates through the proposed benefit and risk routes. The clearest first empirical specification is a dummy-coded condition model with an explicit conversational-versus-UI contrast and a path structure in which conversational guidance increases personalization, lowers cognitive load, and raises confidence while simultaneously decreasing perceived transparency.

The analysis proceeds in a staged but theory-aligned way. The first stage tests whether the conversational condition differs from both the control and UI-nudge conditions on the headline outcomes, purchase likelihood and perceived decision quality. The second stage tests whether the conversational condition increases perceived personalization and decreases perceived transparency relative to the comparison conditions. The third stage tests whether personalization predicts lower cognitive load, whether lower cognitive load predicts higher confidence, and whether transparency and confidence jointly predict perceived decision quality. If feasible, these relations are estimated in a single bootstrapped path model with planned conversational-versus-UI contrasts reported explicitly. If not, the paper preserves the same logic through staged regression and indirect-effect analyses rather than broadening the empirical design into an unfocused set of tests. This sequencing keeps the empirical section aligned with the paper's theoretical claim that conversational AI generates a support route and an opacity route at the same time.

## Contribution
This paper makes three main contributions. First, it conceptualizes LLM-based conversational shopping assistants as a distinct form of AI nudge whose influence is enacted through adaptive dialogue rather than interface placement alone. This moves the treatment of conversational AI beyond the view that it is merely a more powerful recommendation cue and instead positions it as a dialogic form of digital choice architecture. Second, the paper identifies a mechanism-level tradeoff in which the same features that make conversational AI feel more useful and personalized may also reduce transparency in the recommendation process. In that sense, the paper contributes not only a new empirical setting for digital nudging, but also a clearer theoretical account of why conversational guidance may simultaneously support and compromise consumer decision evaluation.

Third, the paper links classic research on digital guidance and recommendation systems to contemporary conversational-AI contexts without treating shopping assistants as an isolated technological novelty. The theoretical value of this move is that it preserves continuity with prior work on decision aids, recommendation agents, and disclosure while still identifying what is genuinely different about LLM-mediated influence. The empirical value is that the study is designed around the theoretically central contrast between conversational guidance and static UI nudging, with the no-nudge control retained as a baseline rather than the sole focal comparison. Framing the evidence in this way allows the results to speak directly to whether conversational AI reconfigures digital choice architecture by combining stronger decision support with lower inspectability, rather than merely asking whether another recommendation aid can increase purchase propensity.

## Discussion
The core implication of the paper is that conversational AI should not be evaluated only by whether it increases downstream purchase behavior. If the theory is supported, LLM-based shopping assistants will appear valuable precisely because they make the decision process feel easier, more tailored, and more confidence inducing, yet those same qualities may mask a weaker ability to inspect how recommendations are produced. The study therefore reframes the central question from whether conversational AI is effective to what kind of decision architecture it creates for consumers.

This perspective has at least two theoretical implications. First, it suggests that conversational AI belongs within the digital-nudging and recommendation-agent traditions, but that it should be theorized as a more dialogic and adaptive form of influence than most prior interface-level nudges. Second, it implies that favorable subjective responses to AI assistance should not automatically be interpreted as evidence of higher-quality decision support. Confidence and perceived personalization may improve even when transparency declines, which means that the consumer's experience of being well guided can diverge from the consumer's ability to evaluate the basis of that guidance. That distinction is likely to matter not only for shopping assistants, but also for broader AI-mediated advisory settings in which fluent interaction can substitute for visible reasoning.

The study also carries practical implications for platform design. If conversational shopping tools increase purchase likelihood by reducing friction and making advice feel personalized, firms will have incentives to expand their use. Yet the same results would also suggest that designers should take transparency features seriously rather than treating them as peripheral ethical add-ons. Explanation cues, visible comparison logic, and clearer disclosure of recommendation criteria may be important because they can preserve some of the support benefits of conversational guidance while reducing opacity costs. From a managerial standpoint, the relevant design problem is therefore not simply how to make AI advice more persuasive, but how to make it more inspectable without destroying its convenience.

Several limitations should shape the interpretation of the first study. The design uses a single product category, a simulated shopping environment, and self-reported process measures, all of which are appropriate for a first theory test but narrower than the full range of conversational-commerce settings. The study is also designed around perceived decision quality rather than objective downstream product fit or postpurchase outcomes. For that reason, the strongest immediate value of the paper is conceptual and mechanism focused: it establishes whether conversational AI produces a support-versus-opacity tradeoff under controlled conditions. Follow-on work can then test boundary conditions across product categories, vary the visibility of explanation features more directly, and examine whether short-run gains in confidence and purchase likelihood persist once consumers reflect on or experience the consequences of the recommendation.
