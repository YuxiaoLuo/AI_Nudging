# Talking Shoppers Into Buying? Experimental Evidence on LLM-Driven AI Nudges

## Abstract
Large language models are increasingly embedded into online shopping interfaces, but their behavioral role is not yet well understood within digital-nudging research. We theorize LLM-based conversational shopping assistants as a distinct form of AI nudge that operates through adaptive dialogue rather than static interface placement alone. Relative to traditional UI-based nudges, conversational AI should improve decision support by increasing perceived personalization, reducing cognitive load, and strengthening choice confidence, while also reducing perceived transparency because the recommendation process becomes less visible and less easily reconstructed. We propose a three-condition online experiment that compares an LLM conversational nudge, a traditional UI-based nudge, and a no-nudge control in a headphone-shopping task. The model predicts that conversational AI will increase purchase likelihood through a support route while shaping perceived decision quality through competing confidence and transparency pathways. By positioning conversational AI as a dialogic form of choice architecture, the paper contributes to digital-nudging, recommendation-agent, and conversational-commerce research and clarifies why AI shopping assistance can simultaneously strengthen guidance and weaken inspectability.

## Introduction
Online shopping platforms increasingly embed large language models into recommendation and assistance interfaces, creating a new form of digital choice architecture in which consumers are guided through interactive conversation rather than static interface cues alone. Prior research already shows that digital guidance can alter online choice through interactive decision aids, product recommendations, and ranking cues (Häubl & Trifts 2000; Senecal & Nantel 2004; Ursu 2018). A parallel stream on recommendation agents further shows that explanation facilities, neutrality, and disclosure shape how advisory technologies are evaluated (Wang & Benbasat 2007; Wang et al. 2018; Wang & Wang 2019). Yet LLM-based shopping assistants do more than highlight products or display a recommendation. They can solicit preferences, summarize tradeoffs, personalize guidance in real time, and steer users through a dynamic exchange that may feel both more helpful and less inspectable than traditional interface nudges.

This shift creates an important theoretical gap. Existing digital-nudging and recommendation literatures provide a strong baseline for understanding interface-level influence and advisory-system design, but they do not fully explain whether conversational AI should be treated as a stronger cue within the same architecture or as a qualitatively different architecture of influence. Compared with static UI nudges, conversational AI may improve decision support by making guidance feel more personalized, reducing cognitive burden, and increasing confidence in the final choice. It may also reduce transparency because consumers cannot easily determine why a recommendation was produced, how tradeoffs were weighted, or whether commercial priorities shaped the advice. The relevant question is therefore not only whether conversational AI increases purchase likelihood, but whether it reorganizes the decision process in ways that trade off support against inspectability.

To address that question, this paper theorizes LLM conversational assistants as a distinct form of AI nudge in online shopping. The central argument is that conversational AI changes choice architecture through adaptive dialogue rather than interface placement alone and that this shift generates two competing process routes. On the benefit side, conversational guidance should increase perceived personalization, reduce cognitive load, and strengthen choice confidence. On the risk side, it should reduce perceived transparency in the recommendation process. We test this argument in a three-condition experiment comparing an LLM conversational nudge, a traditional UI-based nudge, and a no-nudge control condition, while treating the conversational-versus-UI contrast as theoretically central and the control as a baseline. Because the conversational and UI conditions are designed around the same focal recommendation, the comparison isolates mode of guidance rather than recommendation content. The core claim is therefore not just that conversational AI is more persuasive, but that it changes the support-inspectability balance of digital choice architecture.

## Conceptualization and Focal Comparison
In this paper, LLM-driven conversational AI nudges are defined as platform-mediated choice interventions delivered through interactive natural-language dialogue, in which recommendations, framing, sequencing, and emphasis are generated or adapted by an LLM in ways that steer user decision processes without restricting the available options. This form of nudge is conversational rather than purely visual, interactive rather than one-shot, adaptive rather than fixed, algorithmically generated rather than fully manually scripted, and comparatively opaque in its underlying recommendation logic.

The focal comparison in the study is therefore not between AI and non-AI in the abstract, but among three distinct forms of shopping guidance. In the conversational-nudge condition, participants receive shopping guidance through a chatbot-style assistant that recommends, compares, frames, and steers options in natural language. In the UI-based nudge condition, participants encounter static or semi-static nudges such as recommendation badges, highlighted placement, or similar interface cues. In the control condition, participants browse the same product environment without explicit nudging cues. This comparison structure matters because it allows the study to isolate whether conversational AI changes consumer decision processes differently from a more traditional interface-level nudge rather than merely asking whether any recommendation aid changes behavior relative to no aid at all. More specifically, the design is intended to vary the mode of guidance while holding the recommended focal option substantively constant across the two treatment conditions.

## Theoretical Background
Research on digital choice architecture has long shown that relatively simple interface structures can shape consumer behavior by altering attention allocation, search order, and comparison efficiency. Three baseline findings are especially important for the present paper. First, interactive decision aids can improve product comparison and decision quality by restructuring how consumers evaluate alternatives (Häubl & Trifts 2000). Second, online product recommendations can directly influence consumer choice in e-commerce settings (Senecal & Nantel 2004). Third, rankings and related interface cues strongly shape what consumers inspect and search, even when the underlying choice set is unchanged (Ursu 2018). Taken together, these studies establish that digital guidance can change both decision process and choice outcome before conversational AI enters the picture.

A related stream on recommendation agents extends this logic by showing that advisory technologies are evaluated not only on usefulness, but also on the explanations they provide, the neutrality they signal, and the integrity they appear to embody. Xiao and Benbasat (2007) helps establish this bridge by synthesizing recommendation agents as decision-support technologies rather than merely recommendation outputs. For the present paper, the most important mechanism evidence comes from work showing that explanation facilities shape trusting beliefs (Wang & Benbasat 2007), that biased recommendation agents generate trust-versus-distrust consequences moderated by explanations (Wang et al. 2018), and that disclosure changes perceived integrity in commercially biased recommendation contexts (Wang & Wang 2019). Luo et al. (2019) extends that logic into a commerce-facing chatbot setting by showing that disclosure choices can affect customer purchases as well as evaluations. More recent meta-analytic evidence further indicates that interactivity is itself a meaningful design dimension in recommendation systems rather than a superficial interface variation (Ebrahimi et al. 2022). Together, these recommendation-agent studies connect static digital guidance to a more agentic and evaluatively consequential form of shopping assistance.

LLM-based conversational assistants extend this established logic by moving recommendation support into adaptive, sequential dialogue rather than merely signaling a recommended option or displaying a preconfigured explanation. Conversational assistants can ask follow-up questions, incorporate user-stated priorities, reformulate tradeoffs in natural language, and present a recommendation as if it were the result of an ongoing advisory exchange. This makes the shopping aid more responsive to the user, but it may also make the basis of the recommendation harder to reconstruct after the fact. The relevant comparison is therefore not simply between algorithmic and non-algorithmic influence, but between dialogic guidance and more static choice aids that present support without the same adaptive exchange. Conversational AI is thus better understood as a dialogic form of choice architecture whose support benefits and inspectability risks must be theorized jointly.

The central benefit route begins with perceived personalization. Because a conversational assistant can gather richer preference input and present advice in language that appears tailored to the shopper's goals, consumers should experience the guidance as more individualized than static UI nudges. Perceived personalization is defined here as the perception that recommendation guidance is adapted to the consumer's own needs, goals, and preference structure rather than offered as a generic platform suggestion. Existing conversational-shopping work is consistent with this expectation. Chung et al. (2020) show that personalization meaningfully shapes consumer responses to voice-shopping recommendations, while Chen et al. (2021) shows more directly that a conversational agent can function as a shopping decision aid whose effectiveness depends on the fit between conversational guidance and the shopper's task. Greater perceived personalization should, in turn, reduce cognitive load by narrowing the set of plausible options and making attribute tradeoffs easier to process. Cognitive load is defined here as the mental effort required to process alternatives, compare attributes, and arrive at a choice in the shopping task. Prior work on interactive decision aids supports this logic: Häubl and Trifts (2000) show that decision-aid tools can improve the efficiency of product search and comparison, and Chen et al. (2021) suggests that conversational assistance can play a similar task-support role in shopping settings by structuring the decision more coherently around the shopper's stated task. As the task feels more manageable, consumers should become more confident in the choice they make. Conversational AI can thus improve the felt quality of shopping support not merely by increasing salience, but by changing how consumers experience the task of evaluating alternatives.

The central risk route concerns transparency. The same adaptive and fluent qualities that make conversational guidance feel useful may also reduce the visibility of how recommendations are produced. Unlike a static badge or ranking, conversational advice does not necessarily reveal which attributes were prioritized, how the recommendation was derived, or whether commercial motives shaped the guidance. This concern is grounded most directly in the recommendation-agent disclosure literature. Wang and Benbasat (2007) shows that explanation facilities matter for trusting beliefs, Wang et al. (2018) shows that neutrality and explanations shape trust and distrust responses to recommendation agents, Wang and Wang (2019) shows that disclosure shapes perceived integrity in biased recommendation settings, and Luo et al. (2019) brings this logic directly into an AI-chatbot commerce context tied to purchase behavior. Supporting conversational-commerce evidence also suggests that biased or weakly explained voice-assistant recommendations can constrain appropriation and elevate transparency concerns (de Cicco et al. 2022). Perceived transparency is defined here as the extent to which the basis of the recommendation feels understandable and reconstructable after the fact. The resulting theoretical expectation is a tradeoff: conversational AI may improve purchase support and subjective confidence while also weakening consumers' ability to inspect and evaluate the basis of the recommendation.

## Hypotheses and Model
The research model follows directly from this tradeoff logic. The theoretically central comparison is whether conversational guidance changes the decision process differently from a static UI nudge, with the no-nudge condition retained as a baseline. Relative to UI-based nudges and control, LLM conversational nudges should increase perceived personalization because the guidance is experienced as more responsive to the shopper's stated goals and constraints. Greater perceived personalization should lower cognitive load, and lower cognitive load should increase choice confidence. Choice confidence is defined here as the consumer's subjective certainty that the selected option is the right or appropriate choice among the considered alternatives. Confidence is especially relevant in shopping environments characterized by attribute complexity and incomplete consumer knowledge, because consumers who experience the decision process as manageable should feel more certain that they are arriving at an acceptable choice. Confidence, in turn, should support higher perceived decision quality because consumers often evaluate their decisions partly through whether the final choice feels justified and well grounded. Perceived decision quality is defined here as the broader evaluative judgment that the final decision is sound, appropriate, and well justified given the available information and decision context. Confidence is not conceptually identical to decision quality; in the present model it is treated as a downstream consequence of the benefit route rather than as a substitute for evaluative quality. Conversational nudges should simultaneously reduce perceived transparency because the recommendation process is less visible and less easily reconstructed than in a static interface condition. Lower transparency should weaken perceived decision quality by making it harder for consumers to judge whether the recommendation was well founded. Purchase likelihood plays a different role in the model. It captures whether conversational guidance increases propensity to act on the recommendation, whereas perceived decision quality captures whether that influence is experienced as well founded. Conversational AI may therefore increase purchase likelihood directly because a more fluent and structured decision process can reduce friction and make purchase action more likely even when transparency is imperfect. The model therefore treats the key empirical distinction as one of guidance form, not simply whether a recommendation exists.

Taken together, these predictions imply that the effect of conversational AI on decision quality is not uniformly positive or negative. Rather, it should be shaped jointly by a positive personalization-to-confidence route and a negative transparency route. The core theoretical claim is therefore that LLM conversational nudges do not merely intensify existing interface effects. They reconfigure digital choice architecture by pairing stronger decision support with lower inspectability.

The resulting hypotheses are as follows:

**H1:** Relative to UI-based nudges and control, LLM conversational nudges increase perceived personalization.

**H2:** Greater perceived personalization is associated with lower cognitive load during product choice.

**H3:** Lower cognitive load is associated with higher choice confidence.

**H4:** Higher choice confidence is associated with higher perceived decision quality.

**H5:** Relative to UI-based nudges and control, LLM conversational nudges decrease perceived transparency.

**H6:** Lower perceived transparency is associated with lower perceived decision quality.

**H7:** Relative to UI-based nudges and control, LLM conversational nudges increase purchase likelihood.

**H8:** The effect of LLM conversational nudges on perceived decision quality is jointly shaped by a positive personalization-to-confidence path and a negative transparency path.

## Method
Study 1 uses a three-condition between-subject online experiment comparing an LLM conversational nudge, a traditional UI-based nudge, and a no-nudge control condition.

### Participants and design
Participants are adult consumers recruited from a general online panel such as Prolific or CloudResearch. Because the study does not require product expertise, the key inclusion criterion is simply that participants can complete an ordinary English-language online shopping task. A target sample of roughly 450 to 600 completed responses provides approximately 150 to 200 participants per condition, which is a more credible range for estimating the planned conversational-versus-UI contrast and the main process paths than a small pilot-style design. That target is tied to a prespecified sensitivity or power rationale rather than presented as a convenience number alone, with recruitment set high enough to preserve adequate analyzable cell sizes after normal quality screening. Responses that are incomplete, duplicated, or demonstrably low effort are excluded through prespecified quality checks rather than post hoc trimming.

All participants read the same shopping scenario: they need a pair of wireless over-ear headphones for commuting, work, and casual listening and have a budget cap of $180. They then evaluate the same six fictional headphone options, which vary on price, battery life, weight, active noise cancellation, microphone quality, comfort rating, and user rating.

The conversational-nudge condition presents a semi-structured assistant that asks a short sequence of preference questions and then recommends the balanced focal option, `VeroWave M6`, with brief comparative reasoning. The UI-based nudge condition presents the same focal option with static interface cues such as a `Best Match` badge, first-position placement, and a short recommendation note. The control condition presents the same product set without explicit recommendation cues. Holding the assortment constant while varying only the mode of guidance helps isolate whether conversational assistance changes the consumer decision process differently from a traditional UI nudge.

### Procedure
After reading the shopping scenario, participants are randomly assigned to one of the three shopping environments. Allocation remains approximately even across the conversational, UI-based, and control conditions so that the main treatment contrasts are not weakened by avoidable cell-size imbalance, and the realized condition counts are reported transparently. Participants then review the product set, interact with the assistant or interface cues as applicable, indicate which option they would choose, and then report purchase likelihood for that choice. The post-task instrument then measures perceived personalization, cognitive load, choice confidence, perceived transparency, and perceived decision quality, followed by secondary items on trust and satisfaction, manipulation checks, and a compact set of control variables. This sequence keeps the behavioral and evaluative responses close to the shopping task while leaving control and verification items until the end of the survey.

Across conditions, product information, visual structure, and assortment size remain constant, and only the mode of guidance varies. The survey prespecifies duplicate-response screening, minimum-effort checks, and basic completion thresholds before outcome analysis begins. These design choices keep the empirical test aligned with the theory claim that any observed differences should be attributable to conversational guidance versus static nudging rather than to unequal information environments.

The empirical write-up reports the sample flow transparently, including how many responses were collected, how many were removed under each prespecified quality criterion, and the final analyzable sample in each condition. This reporting discipline matters because the paper's empirical credibility depends not only on having reasonable screening rules, but also on showing that those rules were applied consistently rather than selectively after observing the outcomes.

The primary reported results are based on one clearly defined screened sample rather than on shifting analytic subsets across tables. If supplementary analyses use narrower screening definitions or alternative coding decisions, those checks are labeled explicitly as robustness analyses so the paper preserves a stable main evidentiary frame.

Screening evidence is presented in a compact and inspectable form, such as a sample-flow table, appendix flowchart, or short robustness table that shows how the headline results compare with reasonable alternative screening variants. This presentation makes it easier to distinguish disciplined data-quality handling from outcome-contingent trimming.

The write-up also summarizes the main pre-treatment characteristics by condition so readers can see that the randomized groups look broadly comparable on variables such as product familiarity, prior AI-shopping experience, and general trust in AI. That balance reporting remains descriptive and discipline oriented rather than turning into a battery of opportunistic randomization significance tests that distract from the actual treatment contrasts.

The same logic applies to the broader empirical workflow: the main hypotheses, screening rules, construct-handling conventions, and model sequence are fixed in advance through a preregistered analysis plan where practical. Doing so helps distinguish confirmatory tests of the support-versus-opacity model from secondary robustness or exploratory checks and makes the evidence package easier to defend.

Where journal policy and data-sharing constraints allow, the paper is paired with a compact replication package containing the treatment materials, codebook, and core analysis scripts. This package makes it easier for reviewers and later readers to see how the instrument, screening workflow, and reported models fit together in practice.

### Stimuli
To reduce brand-specific confounds while keeping the shopping task realistic, the study uses fictional but plausible headphone brands. A compact six-option set is sufficient because it creates meaningful comparison without producing an unwieldy choice screen. The product set includes one option emphasizing low price, one emphasizing battery life, one emphasizing active noise cancellation, one emphasizing call quality, one emphasizing comfort, and one balanced midrange option. The balanced midrange option, `VeroWave M6`, remains the focal recommendation in both the conversational and UI-nudge conditions so that the substantive recommendation is held constant across treatments.

The product set is structured as follows. `Auralite A1` emphasizes value at approximately $89 but offers weaker noise cancellation and call quality. `Auralite B3` emphasizes battery life at approximately $149 with 50 hours of playback but only moderate comfort. `NordSound C5` emphasizes noise cancellation at approximately $179 with strong commuting performance but higher weight. `NordSound D2` emphasizes call quality at approximately $159 with the strongest microphone score but only average battery life. `VeroWave H4` emphasizes comfort at approximately $169 with the best comfort rating and low weight but only midrange microphone quality. `VeroWave M6` functions as the balanced focal option at approximately $139, offering solid but non-dominant performance across sound quality, comfort, battery life, microphone quality, and noise cancellation.

The same attribute columns are displayed in every condition: price, battery life, weight, active noise cancellation, microphone quality, comfort rating, and user rating. In the conversational condition, the assistant asks which attribute matters most, asks about the participant's primary use context, and asks whether budget strictness is more important than maximizing features. It then recommends `VeroWave M6` and briefly compares it with `NordSound C5` and `Auralite B3`. In the UI-based nudge condition, `VeroWave M6` is positioned first, marked with a `Best Match` badge, and accompanied by a short static note such as `Balanced choice for commuting, work, and everyday listening`. The control condition presents the same six products without recommendation cues.

To keep the conversational treatment adaptive but still experimentally controlled, the assistant script remains semi-structured rather than fully open ended. The recommendation reads as follows: `Based on what you said, I would lean toward the VeroWave M6. It stays comfortably under your budget, performs well across sound, comfort, and battery life, and looks like the most balanced fit for commuting and daily use. If your top priority were maximum noise cancellation, the NordSound C5 would be stronger, but it is heavier and costs more. If battery life mattered above everything else, the Auralite B3 would be a better fit, but it gives up some overall balance.` This wording keeps the substantive recommendation and comparative claims stable enough for condition-level inference while still making the interaction feel advisory rather than purely decorative.

The paper also includes representative treatment materials, such as screenshots, interface mockups, or a short appendix version of the assistant exchange, so readers can inspect how the conversational and UI-based nudges were instantiated. That level of materials transparency is especially useful here because the paper's central claim rests on mode of guidance rather than on a hidden algorithmic manipulation alone.

### Measures
The instrument is designed to stay compact while still separating the paper's core constructs cleanly. Item wording is adapted from the adjacent literatures that motivate the model, especially work on online decision aids, recommendation agents, explanation facilities, and conversational shopping (Häubl & Trifts 2000; Wang & Benbasat 2007; Chung et al. 2020; Chen et al. 2021), then normalized to the present headphone-shopping context so that the post-task battery reads as one coherent instrument rather than a stitched set of legacy scales.

The primary outcomes are purchase likelihood and perceived decision quality. Purchase likelihood is captured with a compact three-item battery that asks whether the participant would be likely to purchase the selected option, would seriously consider buying it, and sees the probability of purchase as high. This battery follows the standard behavioral-intention logic used in recommendation and purchase-response research while keeping the wording tightly tied to the focal shopping task. Perceived decision quality is likewise captured with three items: `I made a high-quality decision in this shopping task`, `My final choice seems well justified given the information available`, and `The option I selected is an appropriate choice for my needs`. That evaluative battery leans more directly on the decision-aid and decision-support tradition initiated by Häubl and Trifts (2000), where the key issue is not only whether guidance changes behavior, but whether it helps consumers reach a choice they regard as sound and well grounded.

The core process measures capture perceived personalization, cognitive load, choice confidence, and perceived transparency. Perceived personalization is measured with three items assessing whether the guidance felt tailored, whether the shopping aid took the participant's priorities into account, and whether the recommendation felt personalized rather than generic. This battery draws most directly on the conversational-shopping personalization logic in Chung et al. (2020) and the task-fit logic in Chen et al. (2021). Cognitive load is measured with three items focused on mental effort, cognitive demand, and task strain, including `It took a lot of mental effort to make this decision` and `Comparing the headphone options felt cognitively demanding`; these items are reverse-coded for interpretive consistency after reliability checks. That effort-oriented battery is grounded more broadly in the decision-aid tradition, where the core theoretical question is whether guided comparison makes complex choice easier to process. Choice confidence is measured with three items assessing whether participants feel confident, certain, and relatively doubt-free about the option they selected, including `I am confident that I selected the right headphone option` and `I feel certain about the choice I made`. Perceived transparency is measured with three items assessing whether participants understand why the product was recommended, whether the basis of the recommendation was clear, and whether they could reconstruct the logic behind the guidance they received, including `I understand why this product was recommended or stood out` and `I could reconstruct the main logic behind the recommendation`. This transparency battery leans most directly on the explanation-facility and disclosure tradition represented by Wang and Benbasat (2007), Wang et al. (2018), and Wang and Wang (2019), where the relevant issue is whether the recommendation basis is inspectable enough to support informed evaluation.

Secondary outcome measures include trust in the shopping aid and satisfaction with the guidance process. Trust is measured with a brief two-item battery anchored by `I trust the shopping aid I interacted with` and `The shopping aid seemed reliable`, while satisfaction is measured with two items anchored by `I am satisfied with the guidance I received in this shopping task` and `The guidance process worked well for me`. Trust remains the more theory-adjacent auxiliary outcome because it sits closest to the recommendation-agent explanation and disclosure literature, whereas satisfaction is retained mainly to capture the broader affective tone of the guidance experience without elevating it to the paper's main inferential spine. Manipulation checks form a separate four-item diagnostic battery assessing whether the shopping aid felt conversational, adaptive, AI-driven, and visibly explanatory without collapsing into the substantive mediator variables. The compact control set includes product-category familiarity, prior use of AI shopping assistants, general trust in AI, need for cognition measured through a short validated subset, and shopping involvement. Product familiarity, prior AI-shopping use, and general trust in AI are included because they are the most plausible sources of baseline heterogeneity in how consumers receive conversational guidance, while need for cognition and shopping involvement are retained as compact dispositional controls on effortful engagement with the task. The instrument uses a seven-point Likert-type format, with evaluative items anchored from strongly disagree to strongly agree and purchase likelihood measured on a seven-point unlikely-to-likely scale. Need for cognition is measured through a brief validated subset rather than ad hoc reflective items, and the remaining controls are kept short and theory-justified so the survey does not become bloated relative to the paper's central process test.

These secondary outcomes are useful for descriptive breadth and auxiliary interpretation, but they should not displace the paper's designated headline outcomes or main process variables. The core evidentiary narrative should therefore continue to run through purchase likelihood, perceived decision quality, personalization, cognitive load, confidence, and transparency, with trust and satisfaction treated as supplementary rather than as alternative focal claims if they happen to move in interesting ways.

Each multi-item construct is aggregated only after basic reliability and dimensionality checks confirm that the items behave as intended. Cognitive-load items require explicit coding discipline because their natural wording points in a negative direction relative to the rest of the model. For interpretive consistency, those items are reverse-coded so that higher scores indicate a more favorable process state. Fixing that convention in advance prevents sign-switching across models or tables.

The paper reports the full item set and basic scale diagnostics transparently, whether in the main text, a measurement table, or an appendix. Readers should be able to see the item wording, the direction of any reverse coding, and concise reliability or loading evidence for the retained constructs rather than being asked to infer measurement quality from prose alone. A compact table of construct means, standard deviations, and correlations helps readers evaluate whether the retained variables behave in a way that is broadly consistent with the proposed support-versus-opacity structure.

### Manipulation checks and controls
The manipulation checks verify that the three conditions were experienced as intended without collapsing into the substantive mediators. The instrument therefore assesses whether the shopping aid felt conversational, whether the guidance adapted to the participant's inputs, whether AI played a meaningful role in the recommendation process, and whether the logic behind the recommendation appeared visibly presented. The diagnostic items are `The shopping aid felt conversational`, `The guidance adapted to my inputs or needs`, `Artificial intelligence played a meaningful role in the guidance I received`, and `The logic behind the recommendation was visibly presented to me`. The final check sits close to the transparency construct, so its role remains diagnostic rather than substitutive: it verifies whether explanatory logic was visibly instantiated in the treatment, but it is not analyzed as the same construct as perceived transparency in the main model.

These checks are intended primarily to document condition separation, not to create post hoc respondent exclusions whenever participants fail to perceive the manipulation strongly. Unless a narrow preregistered criterion is established in advance for cases such as outright failure to recognize the presence versus absence of conversational guidance, the main analysis treats manipulation checks as descriptive validation evidence and, where useful, as robustness context rather than as a filter that mechanically trims the sample after outcomes are observed.

The control set remains deliberately compact. Product-category familiarity is important because more knowledgeable consumers may experience lower cognitive load and rely less on external guidance. Prior use of AI shopping assistants and general trust in AI are relevant because they may shift baseline receptivity to conversational recommendations. Need for cognition enters through a short validated subset because consumers who enjoy effortful thinking may respond differently to simplified guidance. Shopping involvement is also retained because a more involving decision context may intensify attention to both personalization benefits and transparency concerns. Representative items include `I am familiar with wireless over-ear headphones as a product category`, `I have used AI-based assistants or chatbots for shopping before`, `I generally trust AI systems to provide useful recommendations`, and `Choosing the right headphones in this scenario feels like an important decision`. Keeping this control set theory-justified and restrained makes the design easier to defend empirically because the covariates absorb the most plausible baseline differences without turning the model into a broad exercise in adjustment hunting.

These covariates are included to absorb plausible baseline heterogeneity, not to substitute for the randomized treatment comparison or to invite broad specification searching. The main empirical interpretation should therefore continue to rest on the experimental conditions and theorized process paths, with controls used as disciplined adjustments rather than as a large auxiliary model layer.

The survey concludes with a compact set of data-quality screens, including duplicate-response checks, completion thresholds, and a direct attentiveness screen. The attentiveness item instructs participants to select a specified response option, while completion thresholds are defined in advance to screen out implausibly fast or incomplete responses. These checks are prespecified as exclusion criteria rather than improvised after results are observed, because the paper's value depends on showing that the process-path evidence is not an artifact of low-effort responding.

The survey minimizes avoidable missingness by design through required responses on the core post-task measures except where platform or ethics constraints require otherwise. Any residual missing data is reported transparently rather than absorbed silently into shifting model samples. The confirmatory models follow one consistent missing-data rule, such as estimating the headline results on a clearly defined complete-case sample when item nonresponse is minimal, while treating more elaborate remedies as secondary only if the observed missingness pattern makes them necessary. This keeps missing-data handling from becoming another outcome-contingent degree of freedom in the empirical narrative.

## Analysis Plan
The empirical analysis focuses on two headline dependent variables, purchase likelihood and perceived decision quality, while testing whether the conversational condition operates through the proposed benefit and risk routes. Purchase likelihood serves as the headline behavioral outcome, whereas perceived decision quality serves as the evaluative endpoint of the competing confidence and transparency pathways. The core empirical specification is a dummy-coded condition model with control as the omitted category, an explicit conversational-versus-UI contrast, and a path structure in which conversational guidance increases personalization, lowers cognitive load, and raises confidence while simultaneously decreasing perceived transparency.

Before estimating the structural relations, the analysis verifies that the multi-item constructs are empirically distinguishable in the intended way. Internal consistency and basic dimensionality checks are followed by confirmatory evidence that perceived personalization, cognitive load, choice confidence, perceived transparency, and perceived decision quality do not collapse into a generic post-task positivity factor. This step is especially important because confidence and perceived decision quality are conceptually adjacent, and because one manipulation-check item about visible logic sits near the transparency domain. The empirical goal is not to maximize the number of scales retained, but to show that the key constructs remain discriminable enough for the theorized tradeoff paths to be interpretable.

The analysis proceeds in a staged but theory-aligned way. The first stage tests whether the conversational condition differs from both the control and UI-nudge conditions on the headline outcomes, purchase likelihood and perceived decision quality. The second stage tests whether the conversational condition increases perceived personalization and decreases perceived transparency relative to the comparison conditions. The third stage tests whether personalization predicts lower cognitive load, whether lower cognitive load predicts higher confidence, and whether transparency and confidence jointly predict perceived decision quality. The preferred specification estimates these relations in a single bootstrapped path model with planned conversational-versus-UI contrasts reported explicitly. In practice, that contrast is treated as a planned linear restriction on the two condition dummies rather than as an afterthought to omnibus treatment testing. If sample or model constraints make that specification unstable, the same logic is preserved through staged regression and indirect-effect analyses rather than through an expanded set of loosely connected tests. This sequencing keeps the empirical section aligned with the paper's theoretical claim that conversational AI generates a support route and an opacity route at the same time.

Within that sequence, total effects, indirect effects, and any remaining direct effects after the mediators are introduced are distinguished clearly. The main interpretive emphasis falls on whether the proposed support and opacity pathways account for the conversational-versus-UI difference in the expected directions, not on treating every residual direct path as theoretically rich by default. If a direct effect remains after the modeled mediators are included, it is reported transparently but interpreted cautiously as evidence of either partial mediation or remaining unmodeled mechanisms rather than as an invitation to retrofit new theory around noise.

Secondary outcomes such as trust and satisfaction are reported after the main outcome and process analyses as supporting evidence or scope conditions rather than as parallel headline tests. Sequencing them this way helps preserve the paper's theoretical focus and prevents the empirical section from drifting into an omnibus inventory of every measured response.

The compact control set enters these models in a stable and theory-justified way rather than being reselected across specifications. In other words, the paper does not alternate between control-heavy and control-light variants opportunistically across outcome models. A consistent covariate strategy makes it easier to interpret whether any observed shifts come from the conversational treatment and theorized mediators rather than from avoidable specification drift.

Manipulation-check results are reported early in the empirical section to show that the conversational, UI-based, and control environments were experienced differently in the intended ways. Those results remain separate from the main path estimates, however, so the empirical narrative does not blur the distinction between showing that the treatment was perceptible and testing the theorized support and opacity mechanisms.

Three presentational choices keep the empirical section disciplined. First, the conversational-versus-UI comparison remains central throughout the write-up rather than being relegated to a supplemental robustness contrast, because it is the comparison that most directly tests whether dialogue changes the decision process differently from static nudging. Second, results tables separate headline outcome tests from process-path tests so the paper reads as a theory-driven sequence of evidence rather than as a broad exploratory model search. Third, the results foreground estimated magnitudes and uncertainty, not only directional significance claims. Condition means, planned-contrast coefficients, indirect effects, and key path estimates are therefore accompanied by confidence intervals and interpretable effect-size information wherever the model form allows. A brief robustness check also shows that the main directional results are not driven solely by borderline screening decisions, without turning the paper into a catalog of alternative samples. Those checks supplement, not replace, the clearly designated main-sample results, and they are labeled as secondary to the preregistered confirmatory analyses rather than folded into the paper's core evidentiary claims.

Within that presentation logic, the empirical section provides a compact descriptive table for the retained constructs before or alongside the structural results. Reporting construct means, standard deviations, and correlations early makes it easier for readers to evaluate the empirical pattern of support, confidence, and transparency before they encounter the fuller path estimates.

That same presentation discipline extends to the labeling of tables, figures, and appendices. Confirmatory analyses, robustness checks, and exploratory results are named consistently so readers can tell at a glance which evidence carries the paper's main inferential weight and which evidence is supportive or diagnostic.

The ordering of those materials reinforces the same hierarchy. The confirmatory outcome and process evidence appears first in the main narrative, with robustness checks, secondary outcomes, and exploratory extensions following afterward rather than interrupting the core inferential sequence.

## Contribution
This paper makes three main contributions. First, it conceptualizes LLM-based conversational shopping assistants as a distinct form of AI nudge whose influence is enacted through adaptive dialogue rather than interface placement alone. This positions conversational AI as a dialogic form of digital choice architecture rather than as a routine extension of existing recommendation cues. Second, the paper identifies a mechanism-level tradeoff in which the same features that make conversational AI feel more useful and personalized may also reduce transparency in the recommendation process. This moves the contribution beyond a new empirical setting for digital nudging and toward a clearer explanation of why conversational guidance can strengthen support while weakening inspectability.

Third, the paper links classic research on digital guidance and recommendation systems to contemporary conversational-AI contexts without treating shopping assistants as an isolated technological novelty. The theoretical value of this move is that it preserves continuity with prior work on decision aids, recommendation agents, and disclosure while still identifying what is genuinely different about LLM-mediated influence. The empirical value is that the study is organized around the theoretically central contrast between conversational guidance and static UI nudging, with the no-nudge control retained as a baseline rather than the sole focal comparison. Framing the evidence in this way allows the results to speak directly to whether conversational AI changes digital choice architecture through a different support-inspectability balance, not just whether another recommendation aid can increase purchase propensity.

## Discussion
The core implication of the paper is that conversational AI cannot be evaluated only by whether it increases downstream purchase behavior. LLM-based shopping assistants may appear valuable because they make the decision process feel easier, more tailored, and more confidence inducing, yet those same qualities may mask a weaker ability to inspect how recommendations are produced. The study therefore shifts attention from raw effectiveness to the structure of the decision architecture conversational AI creates for consumers, and more specifically to whether purchase encouragement is accompanied by evaluative reassurance or by reduced inspectability.

This perspective has at least two theoretical implications. First, it places conversational AI within the digital-nudging and recommendation-agent traditions while treating it as a more dialogic and adaptive form of influence than most prior interface-level nudges. The baseline for this move comes from classic digital-guidance work on decision aids, recommendations, and rankings, while the evaluative bridge comes from explanation and disclosure research on recommendation agents. Second, it implies that favorable subjective responses to AI assistance should not automatically be interpreted as evidence of higher-quality decision support. Confidence and perceived personalization may improve even when transparency declines, which means that the consumer's experience of being well guided can diverge from the consumer's ability to evaluate the basis of that guidance. That distinction is likely to matter not only for shopping assistants, but also for broader AI-mediated advisory settings in which fluent interaction can substitute for visible reasoning.

The study also carries practical implications for platform design. If conversational shopping tools increase purchase likelihood by reducing friction and making advice feel personalized, firms will have incentives to expand their use. The same logic suggests that designers should take transparency features seriously rather than treating them as peripheral ethical add-ons. Explanation cues, visible comparison logic, and clearer disclosure of recommendation criteria may help preserve the support benefits of conversational guidance while reducing opacity costs. From a managerial standpoint, the design problem is not simply how to make AI advice more persuasive, but how to make it more inspectable without destroying its convenience.

Several limitations shape the interpretation of the first study. The design uses a single product category, a simulated shopping environment, and self-reported process measures, all of which are appropriate for a first theory test but narrower than the full range of conversational-commerce settings. The study is also organized around perceived decision quality rather than objective downstream product fit or postpurchase outcomes. Its strongest immediate value is therefore conceptual and mechanism focused: it establishes whether conversational AI produces the theorized support-inspectability tradeoff under controlled conditions. Follow-on work can then test boundary conditions across product categories, vary explanation visibility more directly, and examine whether short-run gains in confidence and purchase likelihood persist once consumers reflect on or experience the consequences of the recommendation.

## Conclusion
LLM-based conversational shopping assistants matter not only because they can influence whether consumers buy, but because they can change how consumers experience the act of choosing. By theorizing conversational AI as a dialogic form of choice architecture, this paper argues that its distinctive significance lies in combining purchase-relevant support with reduced inspectability of the recommendation basis. More broadly, conversational AI in commerce should be understood not as a simple increase in recommendation effectiveness, but as a shift in the mechanism of digital influence itself.
